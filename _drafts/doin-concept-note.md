## DOIN: A Policy Learning System for Digital Opportunity

Over the past several years, I have been developing an idea for a more adaptive approach to digital opportunity policy. I call it the Digital Opportunities Intelligence Network, or DOIN.

The basic premise is simple: digital equity policy should not function only as a reporting exercise. It should function as a learning system.

Today, many communities and policymakers still rely on static indicators, delayed evaluations, and one-time planning documents. These tools are useful, but they do not always help decision-makers understand what is actually happening on the ground, how interventions are affecting outcomes, or how conditions may be changing over time.

DOIN is designed to address that gap.

## What DOIN Is

DOIN is a place-based policy learning system that links infrastructure, adoption, skills, local context, and outcomes in a way that can support better decisions over time.

Rather than treating digital opportunity as a single metric or a static score, DOIN treats it as a dynamic system shaped by many interacting factors, including:

- Broadband access and quality
- Affordability and adoption
- Digital skills and local capacity
- Community institutions and trusted support
- Economic and social outcomes

The goal is not only to measure digital opportunity, but to help communities learn from it.

## Why It Matters

The main challenge in digital equity work is not simply knowing where gaps exist. It is understanding what kinds of interventions are working, which ones are not, and how conditions may be shifting in real time.

A learning system like DOIN could help policymakers and practitioners:

- identify emerging challenges earlier
- connect local experience with measurable signals
- compare interventions more intelligently
- update assumptions as new evidence becomes available
- support more place-based decision-making

In short, DOIN is meant to help communities move from static planning to adaptive policy.

## How It Would Work

DOIN would combine several kinds of evidence:

- quantitative data from public and private sources
- qualitative insight from community experience and practitioner knowledge
- structured knowledge models such as taxonomies, ontologies, and knowledge graphs
- feedback loops that allow the system to improve over time

The purpose is not to replace human judgment. It is to support it with better information, clearer relationships, and more timely insight.

## Why This Matters Now

The digital economy is changing quickly. The policy environment is also changing. Communities are facing new pressures around affordability, adoption, workforce development, artificial intelligence, and the practical use of technology in everyday life.

In this context, a learning system approach may be more useful than a static framework. It allows decision-makers to ask not only, “Where are we?” but also, “What is changing, what should we adjust, and what should we learn next?”

## Proposed Next Step

My immediate goal is to develop this idea further through a small, focused pilot that tests the approach in a real community or planning context. I am especially interested in working with collaborators who can help with:

- research design
- qualitative inquiry
- knowledge organization and ontology work
- data integration and model development

I would welcome the opportunity to continue this conversation and explore whether there is a practical way to develop the concept further in partnership.
