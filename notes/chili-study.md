## 🌐 Introduction: Networks, Place, and Digital Equity in Michigan

When I think about digital equity in Michigan, I don’t just think about broadband maps or funding programs—I think about **networks**.

In network theory, there’s a simple idea called the **“rich get richer” effect**:  
systems tend to reward the nodes that already have the most connections, resources, or capacity. Over time, those nodes become stronger, more connected, and more influential.

That same dynamic is showing up in digital infrastructure and services.

A recent study on digitalisation in Chile found that:
- **Wealthier municipalities consistently offer more digital services**  
- And those differences are driven primarily by existing economic capacity [1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596126000388?via%3Dihub)  

Even in a country with:
- **94% internet access**  
- **89% government services available online** [1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596126000388?via%3Dihub)  

…the system still produces unequal outcomes.

The conclusion is clear:

> Digital systems don’t automatically level the playing field—they can reinforce existing patterns of advantage.

---

## 🧭 Why This Matters for Michigan

This is exactly the challenge we face across Michigan.

Through the **Compass framework**, I’ve been working to move beyond connectivity as the end goal and toward something more meaningful: **digital opportunity**.

Through **EUPConnect**, we’ve seen firsthand how:
- rural and under-resourced communities depend more heavily on digital systems  
- but often lack the capacity to fully benefit from them  

And through partnerships like **DOIN (Digital Opportunity Inclusion Network)**, we’re helping communities translate access into real participation and outcomes.

What the Chile research helps clarify is this:

> The network is not the problem—the **flow of value through the network** is.

---

## 🔄 From “Rich Get Richer” to “Opportunity by Design”

If we do nothing, digital systems behave just like network theory predicts:
- resources cluster in already-strong regions  
- services follow capacity  
- and inequality compounds over time [1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596126000388?via%3Dihub)  

But the study also shows something hopeful:

- People in disadvantaged areas often **engage more digitally when systems meet real needs** [1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596126000388?via%3Dihub)  

That’s the opening.

It means we have the ability to **reshape the network**—not by changing the technology, but by changing how we design and deploy it.

---

## 🚀 A Michigan Opportunity

For me, this is where Compass, EUPConnect, and DOIN align.

Together, they represent a shift:

- from infrastructure → to outcomes  
- from access → to opportunity  
- from uniform deployment → to **place-based design**

Because at the end of the day:

> The same fiber line running through two communities can produce completely different futures.

And as the research reminds us:

> **Place matters most for the people who need digital opportunity the most.** [1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596126000388?via%3Dihub)

This is the work ahead of us in Michigan—not just building the network, but ensuring it works for every community it touches.
